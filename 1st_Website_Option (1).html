CLPS Pathway

CLPS: The Fastest Path to the Moon

Commercial Lunar Payload Services (“CLPS”) is NASA's program for buying end-to-end lunar delivery services from commercial providers to send science, technology, and exploration payloads to the Moon.

Credible first-flight path. CLPS lets specialized payload companies reach the lunar surface without building their own lander, launch system, or mission stack.
Recurring mission opportunities. NASA shows 15 planned lunar deliveries by 2028, creating more shots on goal for drilling, mapping, and resource payloads.
Broader lunar marketplace. NASA encourages commercial payloads alongside NASA payloads so the Moon evolves into a multi-customer economy, not a government-only program.

Why Now

The Moon Is Shifting From Exploration to Infrastructure

The Moon is shifting from government exploration to commercial infrastructure, delivery, and resource development.

01

Commercial Lunar Access Is Here

NASA's CLPS program is creating a recurring commercial delivery market for the Moon, with 15 planned lunar deliveries by 2028 and a contract vehicle worth up to $2.6B through November 2028. Frequent lunar access is no longer theoretical.

02

Technology Is Being Proven in Real Missions

NASA is using CLPS missions to test lunar drilling, volatiles analysis, mobility, and surface operations ahead of sustained human return under Artemis. This is the exact validation cycle needed for SLAM's drilling and mapping platform. The lunar surface is becoming a testbed for commercial capability.

03

The Market Is Scaling Now

The lunar exploration market is projected to reach $19.8B by 2030, driven by rising government and private investment. Separate forecasts show continued expansion in prospecting, infrastructure, and surface systems through the 2030s. Capital is moving toward lunar infrastructure and resource intelligence.

04

NASA Is Deliberately Building a Lunar Economy

NASA states that CLPS is designed not only for science, but to expand the lunar economy and support a commercial marketplace that can eventually stand on its own. That creates a timely opening for specialized companies that supply enabling technologies rather than full end-to-end Moon missions. SLAM fits the enabling-infrastructure layer.

SLAM enters the market at the point where lunar access, mission cadence, and commercial intent are converging — early enough to help define the subsurface data layer, but late enough to build on validated delivery infrastructure and real mission demand.
The Problem

Long-Duration Lunar Operations
Need a Resource Supply Chain

Sustained cislunar presence needs dependable propellant, water, and construction feedstock. The next stage of lunar activity requires local supply infrastructure that can support recurring missions rather than a single expedition.

Need 01
Propellant & Water
Local water ice can be converted into oxygen and hydrogen for propellant. A reliable lunar source gives landers, depots, and surface systems more operating margin as mission cadence increases.
Need 02
Life Support Consumables
Crewed bases need recurring oxygen and water. Local production extends operating endurance and allows agencies to plan larger surface campaigns with fewer Earth supplied consumables.
This is a resource access and logistics opportunity. Institutions funding lunar operations need infrastructure that turns exploration into sustained commercial activity.

Initial Wedge Market

Lunar Water & Volatiles:
The Credible First Market

Lunar volatiles are compounds trapped in polar ice and regolith, including water, hydrogen, oxygen-bearing compounds, and other light elements. They matter first because they can support life systems, propellant production, and recurring logistics for missions operating within cislunar space.

Wedge Thesis

Government agencies and commercial operators have explicitly stated they will pay for locally sourced propellant and water, making in-space use the most near-term and diligence-ready first market for SLAM's resource output.

Lunar Volatiles Explained: Water ice and related volatile compounds are the first practical resource targets because they can be converted into oxygen, hydrogen, life support inputs, and propellant. That makes lunar volatiles a bridge between exploration missions and a functioning cislunar logistics economy.

NASA Artemis / HLS

Sustained lunar presence requires propellant and life support resupply. NASA has funded ISRU technology specifically to reduce Earth supply dependence for crewed operations.

Commercial Orbital Depots

Programs like SpaceX Starship and Blue Origin lunar landers will need propellant in space. Locally sourced lunar propellant at cislunar nodes is a plausible next-generation supply model for these depots.

Helion Energy & Fusion Developers

He-3 priced at roughly $20M/kg, with active reactor development underway at companies like Helion Energy and Commonwealth Fusion Systems, could create a secondary valuable offtake opportunity alongside the core volatiles business.

Defense & National Security

He-3 is also used in neutron detectors for nuclear nonproliferation monitoring, a specialized government market that values supply reliability over spot market pricing.

Business Model

SLAM's Unified Lunar Value Ecosystem

One platform. Three revenue pillars. One reinforcing flywheel.

Pillar 1: Access

Subsurface Access Hardware

Drills, corers, bits, and down-hole instruments that let customers physically reach lunar resources.

  • Near-term revenue from system sales, payload integration, and consumables.
  • Positioned within a planetary corers and drills market estimated at $1.14B in 2024.
  • Also benefits from growth in lunar surface infrastructure and prospecting demand.
Pillar 2: Intelligence

Resource Mapping & Core Data

Core samples, borehole logs, and 3D subsurface models that identify where the best water, metals, and helium-3 assets are located.

  • High-margin revenue from mapping campaigns, data licensing, and interpreted resource models.
  • Lunar resource prospecting is projected to grow from $1.2B in 2025 to $5.8B by 2034.
  • Data becomes proprietary IP that improves mine selection and lowers project risk.
Pillar 3: Participation

Royalties & Streaming

SLAM keeps an economic interest in the projects its tools and data help unlock.

  • Long-term annuity potential from royalties on water, helium-3, and other resource projects.
  • Lunar resource extraction is forecast to reach about $5.15B by 2033 in one market outlook.
  • Even small royalty positions can create disproportionate upside once projects move into production.
Access → Intelligence → Participation

SLAM sells the tools that access the subsurface, uses those tools to generate proprietary resource intelligence, and converts that intelligence into royalty participation in the projects it helps enable.

Subsurface Data Leverage

The economic leverage of drilling and mapping is high because better core samples and logging can materially change project feasibility.

  • A focused drilling + core-sample mapping provider could target hundreds of millions per year if it becomes a leading supplier.
  • Reducing uncertainty in H₂O content can shift a polar-crater project value by $1.5B–$7.5B across typical depth scenarios.
  • Deep cores help characterize regolith variation with depth and prioritize mine sites.

Royalty Economics

SLAM's royalties would be project-specific, tied to sites where its drilling and mapping are central to development.

Tools → Data → Royalties → Better Tools

Each drilling mission increases SLAM's proprietary data advantage, and each data advantage increases its ability to secure high-value royalties. Reinvesting data and royalty income into better tools and deeper mapping strengthens the next generation of contracts and mine participation.
